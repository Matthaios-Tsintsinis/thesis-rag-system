The Apollo 11 mission landed the first humans on the Moon on 20 July 1969. The lunar
module Eagle, carrying Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin, touched down in the Sea of
Tranquillity at 20:17 UTC. Armstrong became the first person to set foot on the lunar
surface roughly six and a half hours later, followed by Aldrin. Michael Collins remained
in lunar orbit aboard the command module Columbia.

The astronauts spent about 21.5 hours on the lunar surface, of which approximately 2
hours and 31 minutes were spent outside the lunar module on a single moonwalk. They
collected 21.5 kilograms of lunar material and deployed scientific experiments,
including a seismometer and a laser ranging retroreflector. The mission returned to
Earth on 24 July 1969, landing in the Pacific Ocean.

Apollo 11 fulfilled the goal set by President John F. Kennedy in 1961 of landing humans
on the Moon and returning them safely to Earth before the end of the decade. The Saturn
V launch vehicle that carried the mission was developed by NASA's Marshall Space Flight
Center under the direction of Wernher von Braun.
