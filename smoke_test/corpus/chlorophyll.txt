Chlorophyll is the green pigment found in the chloroplasts of plants, algae, and
cyanobacteria. It absorbs light most strongly in the blue and red regions of the visible
spectrum and reflects green light, which gives most photosynthetic organisms their
characteristic colour. The molecule has a porphyrin ring with a central magnesium atom
and a long hydrocarbon tail that anchors it in the thylakoid membrane.

Two main forms exist in higher plants: chlorophyll a and chlorophyll b. Chlorophyll a is
the primary pigment that drives the light reactions of photosynthesis, while chlorophyll
b is an accessory pigment that broadens the range of wavelengths captured and transfers
the absorbed energy to chlorophyll a. The two pigments together allow plants to harvest
a wider portion of the visible spectrum than chlorophyll a alone.

When chlorophyll absorbs a photon, an electron in the molecule is promoted to a higher
energy state. In the thylakoid membrane, this energised electron is passed down an
electron transport chain, generating the ATP and NADPH used by the Calvin cycle. The
pigment was first isolated and named by Pierre-Joseph Pelletier and Joseph Bienaime
Caventou in 1817.
