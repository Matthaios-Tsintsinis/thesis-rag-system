The Calvin cycle, also called the light-independent reactions of photosynthesis, is the
metabolic pathway that fixes atmospheric carbon dioxide into organic sugars. It takes
place in the stroma of chloroplasts and was elucidated by Melvin Calvin, Andrew Benson,
and James Bassham at the University of California, Berkeley, in the late 1940s and
early 1950s. Calvin was awarded the 1961 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for the work.

The cycle proceeds in three stages. In carbon fixation, the enzyme RuBisCO attaches a
molecule of carbon dioxide to ribulose 1,5-bisphosphate, a five-carbon sugar, producing
an unstable six-carbon intermediate that immediately splits into two molecules of
3-phosphoglycerate. In the reduction phase, ATP and NADPH from the light reactions
convert 3-phosphoglycerate into glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate, a three-carbon sugar. Some
of the glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate exits the cycle to be used in glucose, sucrose, and
starch synthesis. The remaining molecules enter the regeneration phase, where they are
rearranged through a series of enzymatic steps to regenerate ribulose 1,5-bisphosphate
so the cycle can continue.

Three turns of the cycle fix three carbon dioxide molecules and produce one net
molecule of glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate, consuming nine ATP and six NADPH in the process.
