The Apollo program was the United States crewed spaceflight program carried out by
NASA from 1961 to 1972 with the goal of landing humans on the Moon and returning them
safely to Earth. It was announced by President John F. Kennedy in May 1961 during a
special address to Congress, partly in response to the Soviet Union's early successes
in human spaceflight including Yuri Gagarin's orbital flight one month earlier.

The program ran six successful crewed lunar landings between July 1969 and December
1972: Apollo 11, 12, 14, 15, 16, and 17. Apollo 13 was aborted after an oxygen tank
ruptured on the way to the Moon but the crew returned safely. A total of twelve
astronauts walked on the lunar surface, the last being Eugene Cernan during the Apollo
17 mission. The program also returned 382 kilograms of lunar rock and soil to Earth for
scientific study.

Apollo relied on the Saturn V rocket, a three-stage liquid-fuelled vehicle developed
under the direction of Wernher von Braun at NASA's Marshall Space Flight Center. The
program employed at peak about 400,000 people across NASA and its contractors and cost
approximately 25.4 billion US dollars in 1973, equivalent to roughly 175 billion in
2023 dollars. It remains the only program to have placed humans beyond low Earth orbit.
